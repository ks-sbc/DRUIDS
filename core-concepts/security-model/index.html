    Security Concepts¶

    Security is a fundamental part of DRUIDS. It is not just a technical issue, but a political issue. As we've stated previously, DRUIDS can handle some aspects of security, but it cannot do everything for you. It is important to emphasize repeatedly the lesson that the best form of security is having a correct political line. And while DRUIDS can help provide a framework to develop correct political lines, it is ultimately up to the members of the organization. We want to be clear that although DRUIDS is a powerful system when used correctly, it is not a panacea or a silver bullet. We want to be explicit about this, so that nobody is misled into thinking that DRUIDS is a magical talisman that will make your organization invulnerable to repression.

    What Is Security?¶

    Security is the contradiction between the need to be open and accessible to the masses, and the need to protect our most sensitive information from nefarious adversaries. We have noticed some comrades, when assessing this dialectic, have a tendency to hyper-focus on the possibility of infiltration, and to be overly-paranoid about the possibility of being "caught" or "found out". This is a mistake. If we behave as if our enemy is all-powerful, all-seeing, and all knowing, then we have been defeated before the battle ever began. However, the opposite mode of thinking - that "If we're not doing anything illegal, then we have nothing to hide" is equally if not moreso ignorant! How do we navigate this dialectic?

    Good security is a byproduct of correct politics. Consider the incident of Malinovsky and the Bolshevik Party. Malinovsky was a member of the Central Committee, and was also an Okrhana spy. Despite this level of infiltration, the Bolsheviks were successful in their revolution because they designed their organization so that cadres had to not only uphold a correct politcial line, but also had to be able to implement it in practice. If someone acts like a revolutionary, talks like a revolutionary, does revolutionary work, and avoids reactionary attitudes, then they can be said to be a revolutionary. DRUIDS cannot make you a revolutionary in word and deed. It cannot force anyone to act any kind of way. But it can help design an organization with structures that encourage and promote best revolutionary practices.

    DRUIDS and the KSBC navigate this dialectic by emphasizing that we want to minimize security vulnerabilities while maximizing public accessibility, with public accessibility taking precedence over security vulnerabilities. We believe this min-maxing represents the ideal balance between the two sides of the dialectic. However, we also understand everyone's situation is different, and that there is no one-size-fits-all solution. Therefore, we leave it to each org to determine how they want to implement security policies. This will be discussed more in a future guide.

    Is your organization of a clandestine nature? Then feel free to make use of the L2 classification. With DRUIDS, you could literally encrypt every single document and skip out on the public L0 classification if you so desire. Are you a mass organization who's more interested in the public visibility of your work? Then feel free to make use of the L0 classification and ignore the L2 encryption if that's what is best for your model.

    Methods¶

    Now that we've establish the dialectic of security, we can discuss the methods that we use to implement it. DRUIDS has two means of implementing and practicing security: administrative measures and technical measures. Again, we want to emphasize that good politics trumps both of these. But a computer program cannot do politics for you. However, a computer program can augment your poltiical work.

    Three-Tier Security Model¶

    • L0 (Public): Information safe for public consumption
    • L1 (Candidate): Information for members in training
    • L2 (Cadre): Sensitive information for core members only

    Each security level is segregated into its own directory, completely isolated from the others. Each directory is controlled by different levels of IAP. L0 is considered public knowledge, and we broadast this openly on public websites accessible to the masses - including cops. L1 represents a middle ground between public and private. While we don't require encryption of this information, we do restrict access to it based on an access control list which is managed by the cadre. L2 then represents the most sensitive information, and is only accessible to the cadre. We enforce this technologically through the use of hardware encryption keys. DRUIDS manually encrypts and decrypts file on the fly, and this is readily available in our source code for skeptics to verify.

    We will not go into the specific details of the KSBC specific security logic, but what KSBC will say here is that on the whole, we classify 85% of what we do as L0. L1 is about 10% of what we do, and the L2 classification only applies to maybe 5% what we do. Other orgs are free to implement their own ratios as they see fit. We simply make the tools available to you, but we do not dictate how you should use them.

    Need-to-Know Principle¶

    Information is shared only with those who require it for their work, regardless of their general security clearance.

    Compartmentalization¶

    Separation of different types of information and work to limit exposure if security is breached.
